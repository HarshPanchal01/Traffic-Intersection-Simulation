\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
The primary goal of this project was to simulate a bustling 4-way traffic intersection to understand the delicate balance required to maintain smooth traffic flow. Traffic intersections are classic examples of complex systems where continuous physical movements (cars driving) intersect with discrete events (traffic lights changing, cars spawning). 

By building this simulation from scratch, we aimed to study how these systems reach equilibrium and what happens when they are pushed beyond their carrying capacity. Among the considered outcomes we wanted to measure were:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item How varying the duration of the green light phase impacts the average wait time of vehicles. Is there a "sweet spot" that minimizes idle time for everyone?
    \item How the intersection handles increasing volumes of traffic (arrival rates). At what point does the intersection reach its maximum throughput capacity, causing queue lengths to grow uncontrollably?
\end{enumerate}

To answer these questions, the simulation tracks every vehicle's speed, wait time, and position, aggregating this data globally to evaluate the overall health of the intersection under different configurations.

\section{Inspirations and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
This project heavily draws upon the concepts taught in the CSCI 3010U Simulation and Modeling course, merging multiple distinct simulation paradigms into a single cohesive project:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Continuous Systems:} The foundational kinematics of the vehicles (acceleration, velocity, position) were directly inspired by the Lab 2 (Projectile) and Lab 4 (Mass-Spring) continuous system models, utilizing Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) to govern movement over time.
    \item \textbf{Discrete Event Systems (DES):} The traffic light state machine and the queueing behavior of vehicles waiting at red lights mirror the DES concepts discussed in lectures, specifically relating to servers (the intersection) processing entities (vehicles) from queues.
    \item \textbf{Random Processes:} The generation of traffic is modeled as a Poisson process using inverse transform sampling, a technique explored in our Random Processes lectures to mimic natural, unpredictable occurrences like radioactive decay or, in this case, cars arriving randomly.
    \item \textbf{Collision Detection:} The logic required to prevent cars from rear-ending each other or crashing in the middle of the intersection utilizes distance-based collision detection and spatial awareness techniques covered in Lab 5.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
The simulation was built in Python using Pygame for visualization and SciPy/NumPy for the heavy mathematical lifting. It operates as a hybrid model combining continuous physics and discrete stochastic events.

\subsection{Continuous Kinematics}
Vehicle movement is modeled using a system of first-order ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The state of a vehicle is defined as $\mathbf{s} = [x, v]$, where $x$ is the 1D position along its trajectory and $v$ is its velocity. The derivatives are simply:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dx}{dt} = v, \quad \frac{dv}{dt} = a
\end{equation}
Where $a$ is the acceleration applied by the driver's logic. Rather than using the unstable Euler method, we employed the explicit Runge-Kutta 8th order (\texttt{dop853}) solver from SciPy to integrate these equations, ensuring high physical accuracy and preventing vehicles from jittering or overshooting when braking hard. 

When approaching a red light or a slower vehicle, the driver calculates a safe braking distance using standard kinematic equations:
\begin{equation}
    s = \frac{v_{initial}^2}{2 \times a_{braking}}
\end{equation}
If the distance to the obstacle falls within this braking threshold plus a safety buffer, the vehicle applies maximum deceleration.

\subsection{Stochastic Spawning}
To make the traffic feel natural, vehicles do not arrive at rigid, fixed intervals. Instead, they arrive according to a Poisson distribution. We use the Inverse Transform Sampling method to generate exponentially distributed time gaps between arrivals. Given a desired average arrival rate $\lambda$ (vehicles per second) and a uniform random variable $U \in [0, 1)$, the time $t$ until the next vehicle spawns is calculated as:
\begin{equation}
    t = -\frac{1}{\lambda} \ln(1 - U)
\end{equation}
This mathematical approach beautifully recreates the "clumping" effect seen in real-world traffic, where three cars might arrive back-to-back, followed by five seconds of empty road.

\subsection{Collision Avoidance and Intersection Logic}
Early iterations of the simulation suffered from vehicles "cutting corners" and side-swiping each other during turns because we used 2D Euclidean distance to measure the gap between cars. We fixed this by unrolling the curved paths into 1D trajectories. The distance to the car ahead is now calculated purely as the difference in their 1D parametric $x$ states along the mathematical arc.

Furthermore, a \texttt{cross\_traffic\_blocking} algorithm was implemented to prevent gridlock. Before entering the intersection on a green light, a vehicle scans the 2D bounding box of the intersection. If opposing traffic is stuck in the middle (moving $< 15$ px/s), the vehicle yields at the stop line, mimicking real-world "do not block the box" laws.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
To gather statistically significant data, we bypassed the Pygame rendering loop and ran the simulation in a "headless" mode. We conducted two primary experiments, each running a simulated 5-minute time block.

\subsection{Experiment 1: Varying Green Light Duration}
In the first experiment, we locked the arrival rate at a steady 0.5 vehicles/second and varied the green light duration from 5 seconds up to 60 seconds.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../analytics/plots/plt_exp1_wait_time.png}
    \caption{Average Wait Time vs. Green Light Duration}
\end{figure}

The data reveals a clear optimization curve. Very short green lights ($<10$s) cause slight inefficiencies because a larger percentage of the cycle is wasted on the yellow/all-red clearance phases. The absolute optimum "sweet spot" is at exactly 20 seconds, resulting in the lowest average wait time of roughly 12.4 seconds. As the green light extends beyond 30 seconds, wait times skyrocket linearly. This happens because while one direction enjoys a long green, the cross-traffic is forced to sit idling at a red light for an agonizingly long time, destroying the global average.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../analytics/plots/plt_exp1_queue_length.png}
    \caption{Maximum Queue Length vs. Green Light Duration}
\end{figure}

The queue length graph corroborates this finding. Longer green lights force the opposing red lights to be equally long, causing the maximum observed line of waiting cars to grow from 5 up to 7+ vehicles. 

\subsection{Experiment 2: Varying Arrival Rate}
In the second experiment, we locked the green light duration at the optimal 20 seconds and slowly cranked up the traffic volume from 0.1 vehicles/second to 1.25 vehicles/second.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../analytics/plots/plt_exp2_throughput.png}
    \caption{Intersection Throughput vs. Traffic Arrival Rate}
\end{figure}

This experiment perfectly demonstrates a system reaching its physical carrying capacity. As traffic increases from 0.1 to roughly 0.75 vehicles/sec, the throughput (cars successfully exiting the simulation per minute) climbs linearly alongside it. The intersection is easily handling the load. 

However, around the 0.75 mark, the graph completely flatlines, forming a hard ceiling at approximately 25 vehicles per minute. No matter how many more cars we pump into the simulation (1.0 or 1.25 v/s), the intersection simply cannot physically process them any faster due to the kinematic limits of car acceleration and the mandatory red light cycles. Any traffic arriving faster than this saturation point simply gets added to an infinitely growing queue.

\section{Discussion and Conclusions}
\label{sec:conc}
In conclusion, we feel highly confident in the robustness of this simulation. By treating vehicles as continuous kinematic bodies rather than simple grid-snapping blocks, we were able to observe realistic traffic waves and braking delays. The incorporation of a mathematically rigorous Poisson spawner ensured that our tests were subjected to unpredictable, real-world-style traffic clusters.

Through automated headless experimentation, we empirically proved that our 4-way intersection has an optimal cycle length of 20 seconds and an absolute maximum physical carrying capacity of roughly 25 vehicles per minute. 

We are very happy with how the theoretical concepts from the Simulation and Modeling course translated into a functional, data-yielding engine.
